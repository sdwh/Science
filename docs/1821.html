1821 - How do land topography and seafloor topography differ in shape and scale?

Imagine you're looking at a map of the Earth. Land topography is the shape of the land on Earth, with mountains, valleys, and hills. Now, imagine you're looking at a map of the ocean floor. Seafloor topography is the shape of the ocean floor, with underwater mountains, valleys, and hills.

The main differences between land and seafloor topography are:

  1. Scale: Seafloor topography can be much, much bigger than land topography. For example, the Mid-Ocean Ridge, a mountain range on the ocean floor, is longer than the Andes Mountain range on land.
  2. Shape: Seafloor topography has more flat areas, like the deep ocean plains, whereas land topography has more varied shapes, like sharp mountain peaks.
  3. Depth: Seafloor topography goes down much, much deeper than land topography. The lowest point on land, the Challenger Deep in the Dead Sea, is about 1,400 feet (430 meters) below sea level. The lowest point on the ocean floor, the Challenger Deep, is over 36,000 feet (10,973 meters) deep!

So, while both land and seafloor topography have interesting shapes and features, they differ in scale, shape, and depth.
